On ancient Mars, water carved channels and transported sediments to form fans and deltas within lake basins. Examination of spectral data acquired from orbit show that some of these sediments have minerals that indicate chemical alteration by water. Here in Jezero Crater delta, sediments contain clays and carbonates.


Site

Jezero Crater

The Mars 2020 rover will land in Jezero crater on Mars and explore an ancient river delta and lake beds that may hold clues to past habitability and whether there was life on Mars. The colors represent differences in minerals exposed on and around the delta with include clays and carbonates that could be high priority targets for exploration by the rover. The delta extends approximately 8 km from the wall and out onto the floor of the crater.


LANDING

OVERVIEW

LANDING

Earlier spacecrafts deployed their parachutes as early as possible after the spacecraft reached a desired velocity. Mars 2020's Range Trigger deploys the parachute based on the spacecraft's position relative to the desired landing target. That means the parachute could be deployed early or later depending on how close it is to its desired target. If the spacecraft were going to overshoot the landing target, the parachute would be deployed earlier. If it were going to fall short of the target, the parachute would be deployed later, after the spacecraft flew a little closer to its target while doing trajectory corrections. The module then separates from the parachute, finds a suitable landing spot using its new enhanced cameras and then deploys the skycrane and drops the rover. The booster module then untethers and flies a few hundered meters and crashes.

Find suitable study site

As the Perseverance rover explores Mars, scientists identify promising rock targets. They especially look for rocks that formed in, or were altered by, water. Water is key to life as we know it. Such rocks are even more interesting if they have organic molecules, the carbon-based chemical building blocks of life.

Objective B

Find Compelling Rocks

Some special types of rocks can preserve chemical traces of life over billions of years. Finding rocks that formed in water, have the chemical building blocks of life, and can preserve signs of organics and life is key. Together, they improve the chances of finding ancient traces of microbial life on Mars, if any ever existed. In addition to these special rocks, Perseverance also collects volcanic and other rocks to help establish a record of geologic and environmental changes over time.

Objective C

Collect Rock Samples

Once scientists identify a rock target of interest, Perseverance drills a core sample from it. The rover’s rotary percussive coring drill penetrates about 2 inches (5 centimeters) into the target material. Each sample weighs about a half of an ounce (15 grams). The Perseverance rover places each sealed tube in a storage rack on board and transports it until the mission team chooses to deposit it on the Martian surface. The team uses a strategy called depot caching to determine when and where to leave tubes. Perseverance places one or more large groups of samples in strategic locations. a future mission could potentially retrieve and return them all together. The rover may cache over 30 selected rock and “soil” (regolith) samples.

Objective D

Test ability to produce oxygen

Tests the ability to produce oxygen from the carbon-dioxide Martian atmosphere, in support of future human missions. Carbon dioxide makes up ~96% of the gas in Mars' atmosphere. Oxygen is only 0.13%, compared to 21% in Earth's atmosphere. MOXIE instrument aboard the rover will demonstrate a way that future explorers might produce oxygen from the Martian atmosphere for propellant and for breathing.

Tech Demo

Demonstrate flight capabilities

The atmosphere of Mars is very thin: less than 1% the density of Earth's. A technology demonstration to test the first powered flight on Mars. The helicopter will ride to Mars attached to the belly of the Perseverance rover. The helicopter may fly for up to 90 seconds, to distances of almost 980 feet (300 meters) at a time and about 10 to 15 feet from the ground. That's no small feat compared to the first 12-second flight of the Wright Brothers' airplane.
