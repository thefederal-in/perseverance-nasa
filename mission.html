OVERVIEW LANDING OBJECTIVES

Overview

Launch Window

July 22 - Aug. 11, 2020

Landing

Feb. 18, 2021

Mission Duration

At Least One Mars Year
(about 687 Earth days)

On ancient Mars, water carved channels and transported sediments to form fans and deltas within lake basins. Examination of spectral data acquired from orbit show that some of these sediments have minerals that indicate chemical alteration by water. Here in Jezero Crater delta, sediments contain clays and carbonates.


Site

Jezero Crater

The Mars 2020 rover will land in Jezero crater on Mars and explore an ancient river delta and lake beds that may hold clues to past habitability and whether there was life on Mars. The colors represent differences in minerals exposed on and around the delta with include clays and carbonates that could be high priority targets for exploration by the rover. The delta extends approximately 8 km from the wall and out onto the floor of the crater.


Landing

Overview

Landing

Earlier spacecrafts deployed their parachutes as early as possible after the spacecraft reached a desired velocity. Mars 2020's Range Trigger deploys the parachute based on the spacecraft's position relative to the desired landing target. That means the parachute could be deployed early or later depending on how close it is to its desired target. If the spacecraft were going to overshoot the landing target, the parachute would be deployed earlier. If it were going to fall short of the target, the parachute would be deployed later, after the spacecraft flew a little closer to its target while doing trajectory corrections. The module then separates from the parachute, finds a suitable landing spot using its new enhanced cameras and then deploys the skycrane and drops the rover. The booster module then untethers and flies a few hundered meters and crashes.
